In January 2023, AbbVie's most profitable product lost its U.S. patent protection. Humira — at its peak the world's best-selling drug at $21 billion in annual sales — faced a wave of biosimilar competitors that would cut its U.S. revenue by more than half within two years. Most companies facing that kind of revenue cliff would have made cuts. AbbVie mostly didn't.

Adjusted SG&A held at 20–24% of revenue through the entire transition. The commercial infrastructure — the sales representatives, medical liaisons, and marketing staff that had built Humira into a franchise — stayed largely intact. And by 2025, with Skyrizi and Rinvoq combining for nearly $19 billion in revenue, that bet looked right.

The question worth asking is whether that constitutes good capital stewardship, or whether it reflects something more complicated about how the pharmaceutical industry is structured.

## The Machine Held

The clearest way to evaluate the decision is through the lens of free cash flow. Despite Humira's collapse — U.S. sales fell roughly 54% year-over-year in 2024 — AbbVie generated $17.8 billion in free cash flow. For every dollar the company spent on SG&A (approximately $14.2 billion adjusted), it generated $1.25 in free cash flow.

The operating margin story reinforces it. By 2025, operating margins had expanded 8.4 percentage points year-over-year — not despite maintaining SG&A, but partly because the commercial infrastructure was already built and the new drugs were scaling into it. The cost of building the sales force had already been paid. Skyrizi and Rinvoq were running on infrastructure that Humira funded.

## Middle of the Pack, by Design

AbbVie is not a pharmaceutical outlier on marketing spend. At 23.6% of revenue, it sits between Merck (16.8%) and Johnson & Johnson (25.7%), roughly in line with Pfizer (23.2%) and above Bristol-Myers Squibb (17.4%).

What this peer comparison surfaces is that the pharmaceutical industry as a whole allocates a significant share of revenue to commercial operations. That's been true for decades — industry-wide SG&A historically averaged over 40% of revenue before recent compression. AbbVie at 22–24% is not an outlier; it's a company operating within the industry's structural norms.

## The Prisoner's Dilemma

Here is where the stewardship argument gets complicated. The peer comparison doesn't settle the question of whether the spending creates value — it just confirms that AbbVie isn't uniquely aggressive. If every major pharmaceutical company allocates a quarter of revenue to SG&A, the relevant question becomes: what would happen if they didn't?

Specialty drugs like Humira, Skyrizi, and Rinvoq require high-touch commercial models. Rheumatologists and gastroenterologists need to be educated on complex immunology data. Medical liaisons answer clinical questions. Patient support programs navigate reimbursement. These are real, specialized jobs — not billboards.

But the aggregate picture is harder to assess. If all major pharma companies simultaneously reduced SG&A by 15%, would Skyrizi's market share change materially? Would patients receive worse care? The honest answer is: probably not, or not proportionally. Each company is rational to maintain its commercial presence; collectively, the industry may be sustaining an equilibrium that benefits no single company but that no single company can defect from unilaterally. Economists call this a prisoner's dilemma. In pharma, it takes the form of armies of sales representatives.

## What Makes AbbVie Different

The stewardship case for AbbVie isn't that it escapes this dynamic — it doesn't. The case is that it navigated a rare moment of forced transition without destroying its commercial capacity, and used that continuity to execute one of the largest product portfolio pivots in pharmaceutical history.

Unlike a software company automating its support function or a manufacturer replacing line workers with robots, AbbVie's commercial success depends on maintaining human relationships at scale. The medical liaison visiting an oncologist's office cannot be replaced by a chatbot. The patient educator walking a newly diagnosed Crohn's patient through a self-injection protocol is doing work that resists automation.

In that sense, AbbVie's SG&A is structurally different from Adobe's, which [compressed from 13.1% to 6.6% of revenue as subscriptions scaled](/2026-03-16-adbe-analysis). Adobe decoupled revenue from labor cost; AbbVie's model keeps them coupled. The workers are not being optimized away.

Whether that coupling reflects genuine value or industry-wide inefficiency — or both simultaneously — is the question the data can't fully answer. What it can show is that AbbVie maintained $17.8 billion in free cash flow through one of the hardest pharmaceutical transitions on record, with its workforce mostly intact. That's either exceptional capital stewardship or a well-managed version of a collective action problem.

Probably both.
